Each movie gets a 1 in its genre column and 0 everywhere else. values_fill = 0 ensures missing combinations are zero rather than NA.

The cosine_sim() Function
Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors — if two movies point in the same direction in genre-space, they're similar. It has three steps:

Step 1: sim <- mat %*% t(mat) — dot products between every pair of movie vectors (the numerator). For two movies in the same genre this equals 1, for different genres it equals 0.

Step 2: norms <- sqrt(rowSums(mat^2)) — the length (magnitude) of each movie's vector. For a binary one-hot vector like [1, 0, 0] this is always 1, but it matters when movies could belong to multiple genres.

Step 3: sim / outer(norms, norms) — divides every dot product by the product of the two movies' lengths, normalizing to a 0–1 scale.

The Resulting genre_sim
A movies × movies similarity matrix where each cell is how similar two movies are by genre:

                  super_man  birds_of_prey  iron_man  the_grinch ...

DC movies are perfectly similar to each other (1.0), completely dissimilar to Marvel or holiday movies (0.0). The Weighted Average Formula
r
denom <- sum(abs(neighbor_sims))
sum(neighbor_sims * neighbor_ratings) / denom
It answers the question: "Given how this viewer rated similar movies, what would they probably rate this unrated movie?"

The key insight is that not all rated movies should have equal say. A movie that's very similar to the target should influence the prediction more than a movie that's barely related.

A Simple Example Without Weighting
Say we want to predict anne's rating for super_man. She's rated three DC/similar movies:

birds_of_prey    rating: 5
the_suicide_squad rating: 4  
iron_man          rating: 1
A plain average gives: (5 + 4 + 1) / 3 = 3.3

But this treats iron_man — a Marvel movie — as equally informative as the DC movies, which is wrong.

With Weighting
Now factor in how similar each movie is to super_man:

                   similarity    rating    similarity × rating
birds_of_prey          0.90         5           4.50
the_suicide_squad      0.85         4           3.40
iron_man               0.05         1           0.05
                      -----                    -----
sum (denominator)      1.80         	         7.95

predicted score = 7.95 / 1.80 = 4.42
The DC movies dominate because their high similarity scores give them much more voting power. iron_man's low rating barely affects the result because its similarity weight is tiny.

Why Divide by the Sum of Similarities?
The denominator sum(abs(neighbor_sims)) is a normalizer — it scales the result back to the original rating range. Without it:

# Without denominator
sum(sims * ratings) = 7.95   # not interpretable as a rating

# With denominator
7.95 / 1.80 = 4.42           # back on the 1-5 rating scale
It ensures the output is always a weighted average on the same scale as the original ratings, not an inflated sum that grows with the number of neighbors.
